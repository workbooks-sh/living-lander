  * What they CAN'T say (our wedge — every comparison page leans on these)

    - "What you build is yours to keep." Lovable/Bolt/Replit apps are
      platform-hosted; export is partial or absent. Workbooks apps are
      portable client packages; the runtime is open-source; the desktop
      app runs offline.
    - "It runs on YOUR machine, not ours." The desktop app is the front
      door. Every other cohort member starts with "make an account."
    - "From a personal tool to a shipped product, same artifact." The
      three-layer story (workbooks / runtime / toolkits) beats the
      "weekend prototype → rewrite to scale" arc the cohort all implies.
    - "Open-source, inspectable, self-hostable." Several cohort members
      (Bolt, Replit, v0) are closed-source; only Lovable has a "fork on
      GitHub" surface and it is a partial read-only mirror.
    - "It's not just a front-of-card mockup." Several cohort demos look
      great and don't work past a few screens or users. Workbooks ship
      real logic on a real runtime.

* cohort 2 — Internal tools / data apps (team tooling)

  Members: hex.tech · Retool · Airtable · PowerApps · (also: Superblocks,
           Budibase, Appsmith, Glide, Softr, NocoDB, Baserow)

  Two stories the cohort tells:
    1. "Build internal tools fast" — Retool, Budibase, Appsmith. The
       audience is engineering teams building tools for the rest of the
       company. The product is "dashboard over your database."
    2. "Spreadsheet-database for the whole team" — Airtable, Notion,
       Coda, NocoDB, Baserow. The audience is operators who want
       structure-without-SQL. The product is a table with a nice UI.
  Workbooks is neither. We are a small program with interface + logic +
  data bundled. That is the angle.

    "airtable alternative" and "retool alternative" are the two real

    piece (which is exactly what the shipped post does).

  * What they CAN'T say (our wedge — every comparison page leans on these)

    - "A workbook is a real app, not a database with a UI." Airtable,
      Baserow, NocoDB, Notion, Coda are all "spreadsheet with a
      database behind it." A workbook has logic that branches, views
      that change by role, forms that do something different than
      "insert a row." The artifact is the program, not the table.
    - "It starts on your machine, not in a workspace." Every member
      of this cohort starts with "make an account." The desktop app
      starts with `install`.
    - "From the team's spreadsheet to a shipped product, same
      artifact." The cohort's growth ceiling is "we have a tool, now
      we need engineers to make it a real app." Workbooks skips the
      ceiling — a personal tool is built the same way as the
      fifty-thousand-user product. The runtime scales; the app is
      the same file.
    - "Open-source, inspectable, self-hostable." Retool, Airtable,
      PowerApps, Glide, Softr are closed. Budibase, Appsmith,
      NocoDB, Baserow are open-source but their data still lives
      behind a server you have to run. The desktop app runs the
      app on the machine that owns the data.
    - "From one analyst's spreadsheet to fifty teams, no rewrite."
      The cohort's narrative is "adopt our platform, replace the
      spreadsheet, eventually hit the ceiling." Workbooks' is "make
      a workbook for yourself, hand it to a teammate, run the same
      file on a team runtime when there's a team."
